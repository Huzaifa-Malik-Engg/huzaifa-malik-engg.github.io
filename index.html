Malik Huzaifa

I design and build instrumented mechanical systems — taking them from concept and CAD through prototyping, fabrication, and experimental validation. My current work develops an ultrasonic monitoring system for machining, combining precision fixture design, FEA simulation, and custom data acquisition software.

Erasmus Mundus Meta4.0 graduate across Centrale Lyon (France), Politecnico di Torino (Italy), and NTNU (Norway). I want to keep building systems that measure, sense, and make physical processes visible.

Projects

My master's thesis develops a non-invasive way to monitor what happens at the tool–chip interface during metal cutting, using phased array ultrasonics. I designed the instrumented fixture in SolidWorks and iterated it from 3D-printed prototypes to a metal build, simulated the acoustic behaviour in ABAQUS, and wrote US-Explorer — a complete Python data acquisition and analysis interface for the phased array controller. View the code on GitHub.

In a one-week intensive at TU Chemnitz, our team built an autonomous textile sorting system. An overhead camera detects and classifies items, a 3D point cloud identifies the highest graspable layer, and the UR5 arm picks each item and sorts it into the right bin. My focus was integrating the vision output into a reliable grasping strategy for deformable objects.

Working with VYRK AS, a Norwegian wall panel manufacturer, I modelled their complete production line in AnyLogic using one month of real production data. The model identified bottlenecks, quantified OEE losses, and simulated the effect of adding robotic arms to the line — giving factory management concrete numbers to weigh the upgrade against the baseline.

Note: detailed process data and simulation outputs are not shown, in accordance with an NDA with the industrial partner.

My year-long bachelor's thesis: a portable cold storage room designed for smallholder farmers to transport fresh produce without spoilage. Funded by the Canada Fund for Local Initiatives and deployed in the field with PODA, an NGO working with women farmers in rural Pakistan.

The project placed 3rd university-wide among final year projects, and taught me what it takes to move a design from thermal calculations to a physical build that real users depend on.

I built an 11-degree-of-freedom model coupling a vehicle's suspension and body with a 7-segment human driver submodel, using anatomical spring-damper parameters. Sweeping speeds from 0 to 120 km/h across three road hump profiles, the model identifies the critical speed at which each profile produces peak head acceleration — evaluated against ISO 2631 vibration comfort thresholds.
